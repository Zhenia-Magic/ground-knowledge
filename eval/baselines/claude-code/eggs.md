# Do eggs increase cardiovascular disease risk?

**A calibrated evidence review — July 2026**

## Bottom line up front

For most healthy people, the best current evidence does **not** show that moderate egg consumption (up to about one egg per day) meaningfully increases cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk. This is the majority position of large prospective cohorts, the pooled randomized-trial data, and major institutional bodies (American Heart Association, Harvard). But this is a *qualified* "no," not a clean exoneration, and several genuine uncertainties remain:

- Eggs **do** modestly raise LDL cholesterol in controlled feeding trials — the causal biomarker is real, just small.
- One influential 2019 pooled analysis (*JAMA*) found a **significant** dose-dependent association with CVD and mortality, and it has not been fully reconciled with the null studies.
- People with **type 2 diabetes** are a credible exception where higher intake may carry risk.
- A large share of reassuring egg research is **industry-funded**, which is a real reason for caution about the strength and framing of "eggs are safe" conclusions.
- What eggs **replace or accompany** (oatmeal vs. bacon-and-toast) may matter more than the egg itself, and most studies can't separate these.

I'd put roughly **65–75% confidence** that moderate egg intake has no clinically important effect on CVD risk in the general healthy population, with meaningfully higher concern for diabetics and for very high intakes.

## The four main positions

| Position | Core claim | Strongest evidence | Who holds it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Neutral (majority)** | Up to ~1 egg/day is not associated with CVD in healthy people | BMJ 2020 pooled cohorts + meta-analysis; PURE; RCTs show no hard-outcome effect | AHA (2020 advisory), Harvard, most meta-analyses |
| **Harmful** | Eggs/dietary cholesterol modestly raise CVD and mortality risk in a dose-dependent way | Zhong 2019 *JAMA*; RCT evidence that eggs raise LDL; TMAO/plaque mechanism | Zhong et al.; Spence & Jenkins; some lipidologists |
| **Protective** | Moderate egg intake is associated with *lower* CVD risk | China Kadoorie Biobank (0.5M adults) | Some Asian cohort authors — widely viewed as confounded |
| **Subgroup-dependent** | Risk is neutral in general but elevated in diabetics; depends on the whole dietary pattern | Diabetic subgroup signals; substitution analyses | Harvard; nuanced middle position |

## The load-bearing primary evidence

### 1. The study driving the "harmful" position — Zhong et al., *JAMA* 2019

[[Zhong et al. (JAMA 2019)](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2728487)](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2728487) pooled individual data from **six US cohorts** (ARIC, CARDIA, Framingham, Framingham Offspring, Jackson Heart, MESA), 29,615 participants, ~17.5 years median follow-up.

- Each additional **300 mg/day dietary cholesterol**: CVD hazard ratio (HR) **1.17** (95% CI 1.09–1.26); all-cause mortality HR **1.18** (1.10–1.26).
- Each additional **half-egg/day**: CVD HR **1.06** (1.03–1.10); mortality HR **1.08** (1.04–1.11).
- Associations persisted after adjusting for baseline serum cholesterol — but the egg association **became non-significant after adjusting for dietary cholesterol**, and the cholesterol association weakened after adjusting for eggs + red meat.

**Its key weakness:** diet was measured **once, at baseline** ([[PMC copy](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6439941/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6439941/)), so a single snapshot stood in for decades of eating. The authors themselves flag this exposure misclassification. Single-measurement designs both add noise and can entangle egg intake with an unhealthy baseline dietary pattern.

### 2. The study driving the "neutral" position — Drouin-Chartier et al., *BMJ* 2020

[[Drouin-Chartier et al. (BMJ 2020)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7190072/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7190072/) analyzed **three US cohorts** — Nurses' Health Study (83,349 women), NHS II (90,214 women), and Health Professionals Follow-Up Study (42,055 men) — with diet re-measured **every 2–4 years** (repeated FFQs, cumulative averaging), then folded in an updated meta-analysis.

- ≥1 egg/day vs. <1/month: total CVD HR **0.93** (0.82–1.05) — no significant association.
- **Updated meta-analysis** (33 estimates, 1.72M participants, 139,195 events): per additional egg/day, CVD relative risk **0.98** (0.93–1.03).
- **Regional heterogeneity** (a crux — see below): US **1.01**; European **1.05**; **Asian 0.92 (0.85–0.99)** — inverse.
- **Diabetics:** RR **1.25** (0.99–1.59) per egg/day — a borderline elevated signal.

This is generally the stronger design (repeated measures, larger, direct meta-analysis), but the cohorts are health professionals (limited generalizability), and some authors disclosed food-industry honoraria (dairy, walnut commissions — not egg).

### 3. The large international cohort — PURE (Dehghan et al. 2020)

[[Dehghan et al. (Am J Clin Nutr 2020)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7138651/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7138651/) examined ~177,000 people across 50 countries (PURE + ONTARGET + TRANSCEND). **No significant association** between egg intake and blood lipids, mortality, or major CVD events. Adds geographic and socioeconomic diversity that the US cohorts lack.

### 4. The Chinese cohort — China Kadoorie Biobank (Qin et al. 2018)

[[Qin et al. (Heart 2018)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6241631/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6241631/), ~461,000 adults, found daily egg consumers had **lower** risk of CVD death (~18–28% lower across outcomes). Taken at face value this suggests benefit — but see the confounding critique below.

## The mechanism: what's actually established

This is where the "harmful" side has its firmest ground, and it's important not to overstate the null.

**Eggs do raise LDL cholesterol in controlled trials.** A [[meta-analysis of 17 RCTs](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7400894/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7400894/) found higher egg intake raised LDL by **~8.1 mg/dL** (95% CI 4.5–11.8) and the LDL/HDL ratio by 0.14. This is a genuine, replicated causal effect on an established CVD risk factor — the question is whether an effect this small translates into hard events, especially set against saturated fat, which raises LDL more.

**Individual variation ("hyper-responders").** A [[1998 RCT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9430083/)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9430083/) showed combined-hyperlipidemic people raised LDL substantially on eggs (+12 mg/dL) while others barely responded (+3 mg/dL). Response is heterogeneous.

**The saturated-fat reframe.** A [[2025 crossover RCT](https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(25\)00253-9/abstract)](https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(25)00253-9/abstract) found that **2 eggs/day in a low-saturated-fat diet actually lowered LDL** vs. a high-saturated-fat diet with almost no eggs — supporting the modern view that saturated fat, not dietary cholesterol per se, is the dominant driver. (Note: egg-industry funded — see below.)

**TMAO / gut-microbiome pathway (contested).** [[Spence and colleagues](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8174346/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8174346/) argue egg-yolk phosphatidylcholine is converted by gut bacteria to TMAO, which promotes atherosclerosis, and that egg-yolk intake tracks with carotid plaque burden. **Counter-evidence:** a [[randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8410632/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8410632/) found choline *supplements* — but **not whole eggs** — raised fasting TMAO in people with normal kidney function. So the TMAO-from-eggs mechanism is plausible but not cleanly demonstrated for eggs specifically.

## The most important cruxes and unresolved questions

1. **Does dietary cholesterol matter, or is it a proxy?** Dietary and blood cholesterol are [[only weakly related](https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/food-features/eggs/)](https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/food-features/eggs/); saturated/trans fat dominate. This is why the [[2015 Dietary Guidelines dropped the 300 mg/day cap](https://www.egginfo.co.uk/news/us-dietary-guidelines-remove-dietary-cholesterol-limit)](https://www.egginfo.co.uk/news/us-dietary-guidelines-remove-dietary-cholesterol-limit). But eggs still raise LDL modestly in RCTs, so "cholesterol doesn't matter at all" overstates it.

2. **Substitution — what do eggs replace?** Harvard's key point: eggs eaten instead of oatmeal-and-berries look worse; instead of bacon-and-white-toast, better. Most cohorts can't isolate the egg from its breakfast companions or from the sugary/refined-carb alternative. This may be the single biggest confounder and it cuts both ways.

3. **Regional heterogeneity.** Why are Asian cohorts inverse (protective) while US/European are null-to-slightly-positive? The BMJ authors judge the Asian signal "mainly driven by the egg *consumption pattern* rather than eggs per se" — in China, egg eaters historically tended to be *wealthier and healthier*, so eggs may mark higher socioeconomic status. [[Spence & Jenkins (Heart 2018)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30309867/)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30309867/) argue a true cardiovascular *benefit* of eggs "is most unlikely" and attribute the Chinese finding to confounding and reverse causation.

4. **Diabetics as a real exception.** Multiple lines converge on elevated risk in diabetes: the BMJ meta-analysis diabetic subgroup (RR 1.25), older Harvard cohort findings, and Harvard's suggestion to cap yolks at ~3/week for diabetics. **Countervailing:** the [[DIABEGG RCT](https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(22\)02876-3/fulltext)](https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(22)02876-3/fulltext) (Fuller et al.) found 12 eggs/week caused **no adverse cardiometabolic effects** over 3–12 months in people with prediabetes/T2D. So the diabetic concern rests on observational data that RCT biomarker data doesn't corroborate — genuinely unresolved.

5. **Heart failure vs. atherosclerotic CVD.** Several syntheses note that while stroke/CHD show null associations, ~1 egg/day may associate with **increased heart failure** risk — a distinct outcome that gets lost in "CVD" aggregates.

6. **The unwinnable trial.** No large, long-term RCT with actual CVD events exists or is feasible (you can't randomize thousands of people's egg intake for 20 years). So we are permanently reliant on RCTs of *biomarkers* plus *observational* hard-outcome data — the two disagree slightly, and neither can fully close the gap.

## Evidence-independence caveat (important for weighting)

**The studies are not as independent as a citation count suggests.** The NHS/NHS II/HPFS cohorts recur across the BMJ pooled analysis and virtually every US-inclusive meta-analysis; the same China Kadoorie and PURE datasets anchor the "protective" and "large null" findings respectively. When [[meta-analyses](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8137614/)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8137614/) report 1.7 million participants, they are largely **re-pooling the same handful of cohorts**. Agreement among meta-analyses partly reflects shared inputs, not independent replication. The genuinely independent strands are: (a) US professional cohorts, (b) PURE international, (c) China Kadoorie, (d) the RCT biomarker literature — and these *do* diverge.

## Funding, conflict-of-interest, and source-quality concerns

This is a domain where **funding source is a first-order concern**, and a thoughtful reader should weight it heavily.

- Since ~2010, **more than half of egg/cholesterol research has been industry-sponsored** (Egg Nutrition Center / American Egg Board), per critiques compiled by [[NutritionFacts.org](https://nutritionfacts.org/topics/american-egg-board/)](https://nutritionfacts.org/topics/american-egg-board/) and others. The American Egg Board's explicit mission is to *"increase demand for eggs"* — a structural conflict with disinterested science.
- [[Marion Nestle](https://www.foodpolitics.com/2025/07/industry-funded-study-of-the-week-eggs-2/)](https://www.foodpolitics.com/2025/07/industry-funded-study-of-the-week-eggs-2/) documents that reassuring egg studies (including the favorable 2025 LDL crossover trial) are frequently Egg-Nutrition-Center funded, and argues "research shows that funding exerts influence, whether recognized by investigators or not." Industry-funded studies are more likely to reach industry-favorable conclusions across nutrition science generally.
- **On the other side:** the major *observational* cohorts (Zhong's 6-cohort pool, the NHS/HPFS, PURE, China Kadoorie) are largely **NIH- or public-funded** and not egg-industry sponsored — so the null cohort findings are not obviously bought. The industry-funding concern applies most sharply to the *feeding trials* and to the *framing/PR* around "eggs are heart-healthy."
- **Advocacy on the critical side** also has commitments: Spence and some critics are associated with plant-forward positions, so their mechanistic emphasis should be read with that in mind too.

Net: be most skeptical of strong "eggs are good for your heart" claims sourced to industry-funded feeding trials; give more weight to publicly-funded cohorts and to the convergence of *null* findings across independent datasets.

## Where the institutions landed

- **American Heart Association (2020 science advisory, Carson et al.)**: dietary cholesterol is not a standalone target; healthy people can include **up to 1 whole egg/day**, and healthy older adults up to 2, within a good dietary pattern. [[Advisory](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31838890/)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31838890/).
- **Harvard Nutrition Source**: eggs are "in the middle" — not the best, not the worst; up to 1/day is fine for healthy people; diabetics and those with heart disease should be more cautious; **what you eat with/instead of eggs matters most**. [[Source](https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/food-features/eggs/)](https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/food-features/eggs/).
- **US Dietary Guidelines**: dropped the numeric cholesterol cap (2015) but still advise keeping dietary cholesterol low within a healthy pattern; eggs are included as acceptable.

## Calibrated conclusion

**For a healthy adult, eating up to one egg a day is very unlikely to be an important driver of cardiovascular risk.** The observational hard-outcome data cluster near null; the one significant "harmful" cohort analysis (Zhong 2019) is weakened by single-time-point diet measurement; and the RCTs show only a small LDL rise that a low-saturated-fat overall diet can offset. Confidence: **~70%.**

**The claim should not be pushed further than that.** Eggs are not cardio-*protective* for most people (the Asian "benefit" is best explained by confounding), the LDL effect is real if small, individual hyper-responders exist, very high intakes and heart-failure outcomes carry lingering signals, and **people with type 2 diabetes have a credible reason to limit intake** despite reassuring short-term RCTs. Confidence that diabetics face elevated risk: **low-to-moderate (~45–55%)** — genuinely unsettled.

**The most decision-relevant point** is one both camps largely agree on: the egg itself matters less than the *dietary pattern* it sits in. An egg alongside vegetables displacing processed meat is a different exposure than an egg alongside bacon, sausage, and refined carbohydrate — and most of the epidemiology cannot cleanly separate the two.
