**Sub-question:** Did SARS-CoV-2 arise via natural zoonotic spillover or a research-related incident?

The origin of SARS-CoV-2 remains debated, but the balance of published evidence favors a natural
zoonotic origin centered on the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market in Wuhan. Two 2022 *Science* studies
(Worobey et al.; Pekar et al.) found the earliest known cases clustered around the market and argued
for two separate spillover events from live mammals, and later metagenomic work (Crits-Christoph et
al.) reported SARS-CoV-2-positive market samples enriched for susceptible wildlife such as raccoon
dogs. Analyses of the viral genome, including the furin cleavage site, have been interpreted by most
virologists (Andersen et al.; Holmes et al.) as consistent with natural evolution rather than
engineering, and closely related bat viruses (RaTG13; the Laotian BANAL viruses) show the necessary
features exist in nature.

A minority of analysts continue to argue for a research-related origin, pointing to the Wuhan
Institute of Virology's coronavirus work, the 2018 DEFUSE proposal (which described inserting a furin
cleavage site into a bat coronavirus), the absence of an identified intermediate host, and Bayesian
analyses (Rootclaim; Quay; Weissman) that weight these circumstantial factors heavily. Official
assessments are split: the WHO-China joint study called a lab incident "extremely unlikely," while
the US intelligence community remained divided, with several agencies unable to rule out a
laboratory-associated origin.

In a $100,000 judged debate in 2024 between Rootclaim's Saar Wilf (lab leak) and Peter Miller
(zoonosis), both judges ruled for zoonosis, primarily on the epidemiological proximity of early cases
to the market. On balance, the evidence leans toward natural spillover, though the question is not
definitively closed and reasonable analysts disagree on how to weight the circumstantial lab-origin
factors.
