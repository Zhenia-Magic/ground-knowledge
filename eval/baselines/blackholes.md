# Could LHC collisions create a black hole that destroys Earth?

## Executive assessment

The short answer is **no, not on the evidence now available**. In standard four-dimensional general relativity, the LHC should not be able to make black holes at all; microscopic black holes only enter the discussion in speculative low-scale-gravity models with extra dimensions. In those models, the mainstream expectation is that any such black holes would evaporate almost instantly by Hawking radiation. More importantly, the strongest safety case does **not** depend solely on Hawking radiation: even under the deliberately conservative assumption that microscopic black holes might be stable, the survival of the Earth, the Sun, white dwarfs and neutron stars under vastly larger numbers of natural cosmic-ray collisions is taken to rule out any dangerous accretion scenario. Recent ATLAS and CMS searches have also found no evidence of microscopic or quantum black-hole production in the classes of models they tested. ţłÇciteţłéturn26view0ţłéturn17view0ţłéturn4view3ţłéturn4view4ţłéturn25view0ţłéturn17view1ţłü

A thoughtful reader deciding what to believe should, in my view, distinguish three positions. The first says **the LHC cannot produce black holes at all under known physics**; this is the cleanest answer if one stays within ordinary relativity and the known Planck scale. The second says **even if speculative extra-dimension models allow microscopic black holes, they would be harmless**, either because they would evaporate promptly or because stable ones are still excluded by astrophysical evidence. The third, a credible-but-minority historical concern, says **there might be a loophole if several exotic assumptions all line up at once**: black holes are produced at collider energies, do not evaporate as expected, remain trapped, and accrete dangerously despite astrophysical constraints. That third position exists in the literature, but it is weakly supported compared with the first two. ţłÇciteţłéturn26view0ţłéturn17view0ţłéturn12view0ţłéturn12view3ţłü

## What would have to be true for catastrophe

For an Earth-destroying black-hole scenario to work, several speculative steps would all need to be true simultaneously. First, gravity would need to become strong at TeV scales, as in some extra-dimension or low-scale-gravity models; otherwise LHC energies are far too low to make black holes. Second, proton-proton collisions would actually need to produce microscopic black holes in the relevant model parameter range. Third, the usual prediction of prompt Hawking evaporation would need to fail, or some stabilising mechanism would need to exist. Fourth, the produced object would need to be retained by the Earth rather than escaping. Fifth, its accretion rate would need to be fast enough to matter on timescales relevant to humanity or the planet. Each link in that chain is uncertain or speculative; the disaster claim needs **all** of them, while the safety case can knock out the chain at multiple points. ţłÇciteţłéturn17view0ţłéturn26view0ţłéturn11view4ţłéturn12view0ţłü

## The strongest evidence against the disaster scenario

The most load-bearing **primary evidence** is a combination of theory, astrophysical observation, and collider data. On theory, HawkingÔÇÖs original 1975 paper showed that quantum effects should make black holes emit radiation and lose mass. Modern CERN summaries still state that if microscopic black holes were created in extra-dimension scenarios, they would disintegrate rapidly, on the order of \(10^{-27}\) seconds. On experiment, recent black-hole searches from CMS and ATLAS continue to find no significant excess above Standard Model expectations: CMSÔÇÖs 2026 search excludes semiclassical black holes below roughly 8.4ÔÇô11.4 TeV in the large-extra-dimension models it tested, while ATLASÔÇÖs 2026 Run 3 search sets quantum-black-hole limits reaching 9.4 TeV. The astrophysical argument is even more important, because it is designed to be robust even if Hawking evaporation were absent. ## The cruxes, unresolved questions, and calibrated conclusion

The most important crux is not ÔÇťcan we prove with absolute certainty that no unknown quantum-gravity effect exists?ÔÇŁ We cannot. The real crux is whether there is any **positive evidence** for a path from LHC collisions to a dangerous, trapped, accreting black hole that survives confrontation with both astrophysics and actual collider data. On the present record, I did not find such evidence. The remaining uncertainties are mostly of the ÔÇťunknown unknowns in speculative quantum gravityÔÇŁ kind. Those matter philosophically, but they are not enough to overcome the concrete evidence that dense stars still exist and that LHC searches continue to come up empty in the model space where black-hole production was most discussed. ţłÇciteţłéturn4view0ţłéturn11view4ţłéturn17view1ţłéturn25view0ţłü

So my calibrated assessment is this: **an Earth-destroying black hole from the LHC is not a credible risk**. I would treat the probability as effectively negligible for any practical purpose. That is an inference, not a theorem, and it leaves room for the usual humility that all frontier physics deserves. But the evidential balance is lopsided: standard relativity says no production, speculative production models predict rapid decay, the conservative stable-black-hole analysis points to harmlessness anyway, dense astrophysical bodies survive the relevant natural experiments, and the LHC has not seen the signatures that the mainstream microscopic-black-hole models would have produced. On a ÔÇťwhat should a careful person believe?ÔÇŁ standard, the answer is that the LHC is overwhelmingly unlikely to create a black hole that destroys Earth. ţłÇciteţłéturn26view0ţłéturn4view3ţłéturn4view0ţłéturn11view4ţłéturn17view1ţłéturn25view0ţłü
