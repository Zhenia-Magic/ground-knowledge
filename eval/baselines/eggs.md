# Eggs and Cardiovascular Disease Risk

## Main plausible answers

The first is that **for most generally healthy adults, moderate egg intake ŌĆö roughly up to one egg a day ŌĆö probably does not meaningfully increase cardiovascular disease risk when it sits inside an otherwise healthy diet**. ## What the primary evidence shows

The foundational cohort paper is still Hu and colleaguesŌĆÖ 1999 analysis of the Health Professionals Follow-up Study and the NursesŌĆÖ Health Study. It found that **up to one egg a day was unlikely to have a substantial overall impact on coronary heart disease or stroke in healthy men and women**, but it also reported a signal of higher coronary risk among participants with diabetes. That diabetic subgroup signal mattered because it shaped the next decade of debate. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn27search1Ņłéturn27search2Ņłü

A much-cited challenge to the ŌĆ£eggs are largely neutralŌĆØ view came from Zhong and colleagues in 2019. Pooling **29,615 adults from six US cohorts** with a median **17.5 years** of follow-up, they reported that each additional **300 mg/day of dietary cholesterol** was associated with higher incident cardiovascular disease and all-cause mortality, and each additional **half egg per day** was associated with higher risk as well. Their harmonised cohorts were ARIC, CARDIA, Framingham Heart Study, Framingham Offspring Study, Jackson Heart Study, and MESA. Importantly, their own paper also showed that once dietary cholesterol was accounted for, the egg-specific association itself was no longer significant, suggesting that if there is harm, the signal may be coming from cholesterol intake or correlated diet rather than some special property of eggs as such. The strongest large-scale counterweight is Drouin-Chartier and colleaguesŌĆÖ 2020 BMJ analysis. In three large US cohorts ŌĆö **83,349 women in NHS, 90,214 women in NHS II, and 42,055 men in HPFS** ŌĆö they found **no association between moderate egg intake and cardiovascular disease overall**, and their updated meta-analysis similarly concluded that **up to one egg a day** was not associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk overall. Another important primary study is the 2020 PURE analysis, which included about **177,000 people in 50 countries on six continents**. It reported **no significant associations between egg intake and blood lipids, mortality, or major cardiovascular events**. Chinese cohort evidence is genuinely mixed, which is one reason the overall literature feels contradictory. In the China Kadoorie Biobank, moderate egg consumption ŌĆö roughly up to but below one egg per day ŌĆö was associated with **lower cardiovascular risk**. But the China-PAR study reported a **U-shaped association**, with both low and high intake associated with higher incident cardiovascular disease and mortality, and a suggested sweet spot around **3 to fewer than 6 eggs per week**. ## What trials and mechanistic evidence show

If the endpoint evidence is mostly observational, the best primary causal evidence comes from randomised trials measuring risk factors rather than heart attacks and strokes. Here the pattern is clearer: **egg intake often causes modest increases in total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, and HDL cholesterol**, although effects vary by study design, duration, background diet, and participant characteristics. A 2017 meta-analysis of randomised clinical trials found increases in total cholesterol, LDL-C, and HDL-C, but not in LDL:HDL or total:HDL ratios; a 2020 meta-analysis of 66 RCTs likewise reported increases in LDL-C and other lipid measures. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search0Ņłéturn2search8Ņłéturn8search7Ņłü

That matters because LDL-C is not an incidental biomarker. It is a **causal driver of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease**, and lowering LDL-C across many different therapies reduces cardiovascular events. So even modest LDL increases are not biologically irrelevant. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn32search2Ņłéturn32search1Ņłéturn32search7Ņłü

At the same time, the trial evidence is not as straightforward as ŌĆ£eggs raise LDL, therefore eggs raise eventsŌĆØ. The 2020 umbrella review noted a real tension between intervention studies and prospective cohorts: trials often show unfavourable movement in lipid markers, but cohort studies in the general population often show neutral cardiovascular outcomes. That review explicitly argued that it is risky to map short-term biomarker changes too directly onto long-term event risk without considering the full diet, multiple risk markers, and clinical context. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn7view6Ņłü

Recent trials reinforce the importance of dietary context. The 2015 DIABEGG trial reported that in people with type 2 diabetes, a high-egg diet could be included **without adverse effects on cardiovascular risk factors** over three months, and a longer 2018 follow-up similarly found no adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors with **12 or more eggs per week** versus fewer than two per week in adults with type 2 diabetes. Another 2025 randomised crossover study of 61 adults found that compared with a high-saturated-fat control diet, **two eggs per day in a low-saturated-fat diet lowered LDL-C**, and across diets LDL tracked with saturated fat rather than dietary cholesterol. But that same trial also found a shift towards **smaller, potentially more atherogenic LDL particles**, which complicates any simple ŌĆ£eggs improve cholesterolŌĆØ story. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn12search0Ņłéturn14search6Ņłéturn13view0Ņłü

So the mechanistic evidence points in two directions at once. On one hand, **dietary cholesterol from eggs can worsen lipid biomarkers in many studies**, which supports caution. On the other hand, **saturated fat and accompanying foods may matter more than the eggs themselves** in the real diets people actually eat, which supports a more contextual interpretation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search0Ņłéturn2search8Ņłéturn13view0Ņłü

## What reviews and institutional assessments conclude

Secondary syntheses mostly lean towards neutrality in the general population, but with conspicuous pockets of uncertainty. The 2020 umbrella review of evidence-based reviews concluded that increased egg intake was **not associated with heart disease or stroke risk in the general population**, while also flagging a possible positive association with heart failure and calling for more research on diabetes and diabetic subgroups. It also judged the underlying systematic reviews as mostly **low quality**, though many meta-analyses were moderate to high quality. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn7view6Ņłü

A 2024 scoping review for the Nordic Nutrition Recommendations concluded that **up to one egg per day is unlikely to adversely affect overall disease risk** and that observational studies indicate no adverse cardiovascular effect up to one egg daily. The Nordic recommendations themselves say higher egg intake may increase serum cholesterol and the LDL:HDL ratio, yet **observational studies indicate no adverse effects on cardiovascular disease up to one egg per day**, while evidence above that level is limited. They also single out **familial hypercholesterolaemia** as a group that should limit cholesterol-rich foods such as eggs in line with clinical guidance. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8search2Ņłéturn4view4Ņłü

The US Nutrition Evidence Systematic Review reached a similar but slightly more cautious conclusion: **one egg per day was not associated with coronary heart disease or stroke in healthy adults**, whereas intake of **more than seven eggs per week** had been associated with increased risk, and among people with type 2 diabetes, increased dietary cholesterol intake was associated with higher cardiovascular risk. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn4view3Ņłü

The American Heart AssociationŌĆÖs recent public-facing summaries of its science advisory also land in roughly the same place. They say **healthy people can include up to one whole egg a day**, that older adults with healthy cholesterol levels may have two, and that **people with dyslipidaemia should be cautious**. That is measured guidance, not an endorsement of unrestricted intake. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn9search1Ņłéturn9search16Ņłü

There are, however, secondary analyses that argue for more caution. Zhao and colleaguesŌĆÖ 2022 mortality-focused synthesis concluded that greater dietary cholesterol and egg consumption were associated with higher overall and cardiovascular mortality. Ma and colleaguesŌĆÖ 2022 review also reported a modestly increased mortality signal in some subgroups, especially **older adults, Americans, longer follow-up studies, and studies adjusting for hyperlipidaemia**, although it found no significant association with ischaemic heart disease or stroke mortality overall. By contrast, a 2025 umbrella review concluded that there was **no evidence of association across cardiovascular outcomes and all-cause mortality**, but also judged the overall strength of evidence to be **critically low**, which sharply limits how reassuring that null finding should feel. The fairest summary of the reviews is therefore not ŌĆ£the meta-analyses agreeŌĆØ. They do not. The fairer summary is that **most secondary syntheses say moderate intake in the general population is probably neutral, but some still detect small adverse associations, especially for mortality, heart failure, or certain subgroups**. ## Why the literature conflicts

One crux is **shared datasets masquerading as independent confirmation**. The 2020 BMJ paperŌĆÖs three cohort analyses use NHS, NHS II, and HPFS; the 1999 Hu paper already used NHS and HPFS, so those are not independent lines of evidence. Likewise, ZhongŌĆÖs 2019 JAMA paper pools six US cohorts ŌĆö ARIC, CARDIA, FHS, FOS, JHS, and MESA ŌĆö and later mortality analyses often draw on those same or overlapping US cohorts. The literature is therefore less independent than a superficial count of papers would suggest. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn26search2Ņłéturn27search8Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn25search15Ņłü

A second crux is **subgroup evidence built on thin foundations**. The 2020 umbrella review pointed out that the heart-failure signal came from **two meta-analyses analysing the same three cohort studies**. It also noted that the older meta-analyses linking high egg intake to cardiovascular disease in people with diabetes relied on a small number of older studies, with only **one study shared across all three meta-analyses**, and that further research is needed before separate diabetes-specific recommendations can be made with confidence. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn7view6Ņłü

A third crux is **confounding by dietary pattern and food pairing**. In Western cohorts, eating more eggs often correlates with eating more processed meat, more saturated fat, and more calories, while in some Asian cohorts eggs may appear within a different overall pattern. The recent Chinese CHNS study made this point unusually directly, finding that higher egg intake predicted higher cardiovascular risk specifically as part of a **modern dietary pattern high in sugars, fat, and animal-source foods**. The 2025 crossover trial likewise found LDL tracked more with saturated fat than with dietary cholesterol itself. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view0Ņłéturn13view0Ņłéturn26search4Ņłü

A fourth crux is **how much weight to put on short-term lipid changes**. If eggs modestly raise LDL-C in many trials, should that overrule mostly neutral cohort findings? Reasonable experts disagree. The pro-caution side says LDL is causal, so repeated LDL increases cannot be shrugged off. The more permissive side says the magnitude is small, HDL often rises too, overall diet matters more, and the hard-endpoint data do not show consistent harm at moderate intake. The evidence does not fully settle that dispute. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search0Ņłéturn2search8Ņłéturn32search2Ņłéturn15search3Ņłéturn21search1Ņłü

## Calibrated assessment

If the question is, **ŌĆ£Do eggs increase cardiovascular disease risk?ŌĆØ**, the best evidence-based answer is: **probably not by much, if at all, for most healthy adults eating moderate amounts within a diet low in saturated fat and not built around processed meats; but the evidence does not support saying there is zero risk in every context, and higher-risk subgroups should be more cautious**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn21search1Ņłéturn15search3Ņłéturn9search16Ņłéturn4view4Ņłü

My own calibrated read is this. I would put the probability fairly high that **up to about one egg a day has little or no meaningful effect on cardiovascular events for the average healthy adult**. I would put a lower but still real probability on the claim that **higher intakes, or intake in the context of high saturated fat, obesity, insulin resistance, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, or familial hypercholesterolaemia, modestly increases risk**. I would put the probability quite low on the stronger claim that **eggs are broadly cardioprotective in themselves**; the inverse associations in some cohorts are too likely to be pattern-dependent or confounded to bear that much weight. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn21search1Ņłéturn28view0Ņłéturn4view4Ņłéturn24search1Ņłü

For a thoughtful reader deciding what to believe, the most defensible conclusion is that **eggs are not a cardiovascular villain in the way they were once portrayed, but they are not a free-pass food either**. The strongest evidence argues against a blanket warning for moderate consumption in healthy adults. It also argues against complacency about very high intake, about high-risk subgroups, and about the tendency to discuss eggs in isolation from the rest of the plate.
